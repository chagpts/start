Role Play — Building a U.S. AI OCR Accounting Platform

Context: The team is planning a U.S.-focused AI OCR accounting web platform. They want to understand what LoRA is, why India-specific LoRA models are easier to find than U.S. ones, and how to build a practical system using OCR, Llama, accounting rules, approval workflow, and a QuickBooks-style structure.
PM (Mina):

We want to build a web app that reads U.S. accounting documents with OCR, lets AI organize them, and then pushes approved results into a QuickBooks-style accounting flow. But when we looked at Hugging Face, we mostly found India-focused receipt LoRA models and not a clear U.S. accounting LoRA.

AI Engineer (Alex):

That confusion usually starts because people think LoRA is a full model. It is not. LoRA is a lightweight fine-tuning method that adds task-specific behavior on top of a large base model like Llama.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

So the India receipt parser is basically a general model plus extra training for Indian receipts. From a finance perspective, that sounds like attaching a specialized accounting skill to an existing AI brain.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Exactly. The base model understands language in general, and the LoRA teaches it to do one narrow job well, such as extracting vendor, amount, date, tax, and category from receipts or bills.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

Then why do we see India-specific LoRA models more clearly than U.S. ones? The U.S. market is large, so I would have expected a famous “U.S. accounting receipt LoRA” to already exist.

AI Engineer (Alex):

India has a stronger need for country-specific document models because the vendor landscape is more fragmented, regional utility providers vary a lot, and fintech ecosystems create many different receipt formats. In the U.S., document formats are more standardized, existing accounting systems are mature, and teams often solve the problem with OCR plus rules plus general-purpose models instead of one famous country-specific LoRA.

PM (Mina):

So the issue is not that U.S. LoRA models do not exist at all. It is more that there is no single highly visible, strongly branded U.S. accounting LoRA in the same way that some India-specific models present themselves.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Correct. In practice, the U.S. solution is usually a composed system: OCR reads the document, Llama interprets it, a rules engine turns it into accounting recommendations, and an approval workflow decides what gets posted.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

That makes more sense to me from a controls standpoint. I do not want AI posting directly to the books. I want AI to help us understand documents faster, while accounting rules and approvals control the actual posting.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

Then we should clearly separate responsibilities in the architecture. OCR should extract text, Llama should normalize and classify, the rules engine should determine posting logic, and humans should approve the final accounting action.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Yes, that is the right structure: document upload, OCR, Llama JSON extraction, rules engine, review and approval, then QuickBooks-compatible posting. In that setup, AI helps with understanding, but accounting control stays deterministic.

PM (Mina):

Then our product message should be clear. We are not claiming that AI does accounting by itself; we are saying that AI converts messy business documents into structured, review-ready accounting data.

Senior Accountant (Hana):

And that is valuable on its own. If the system can read receipts, vendor bills, and utility bills, normalize vendor names, extract totals and taxes, and suggest the right accounting treatment, the accounting team already saves a lot of time.

AI Engineer (Alex):

That is why our first LoRA should stay narrow. We should not try to train “all of U.S. accounting.” We should train a document extraction LoRA that takes OCR text and returns structured JSON for receipts, vendor bills, and utility bills.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

So the output should be something like document type, normalized vendor, invoice number, transaction date, subtotal, tax, total, suggested account, and recommended QuickBooks entity. That gives the rest of the platform something stable to work with.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Exactly. And suggested account should still be treated carefully, because different companies have different charts of accounts. Early on, the LoRA should recommend, while the rules engine and vendor history apply company-specific logic.

PM (Mina):

So instead of searching for a magical U.S. accounting LoRA, we should build small task-specific LoRAs for our own product. Receipt extraction, vendor bill extraction, and vendor normalization sound like good starting points.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Yes, and before training anything, we need to define the output schema. If the model returns free-form text, it becomes hard to validate, hard to connect to accounting logic, and hard to push into a QuickBooks-style workflow.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

That means the model is not here to “write a nice explanation.” It is here to reliably produce fields that accounting and engineering can trust, such as vendor, amount, date, tax, and whether the document should become a Bill or an Expense.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

Then the training data format should also reflect that. We can use a chat-style JSONL format where the system prompt defines the extraction task, the user message contains OCR text, and the assistant message contains the target JSON.

AI Engineer (Alex):

That is the right approach. We can start with practical U.S. vendor examples like Starbucks, Uber, Staples, Comcast, Adobe, Verizon, and Amazon, then label them as receipt, vendor_bill, or utility_bill with normalized outputs.

PM (Mina):

But we do not really have a large labeled dataset yet. Can we still start, or are we blocked until we collect thousands of manually reviewed documents?

AI Engineer (Alex):

We can absolutely start. The initial strategy should be pseudo-labeling with rules, synthetic data generation, and human correction logs rather than waiting for a perfect labeled dataset.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

So for example, we can use regex and accounting rules to pull out invoice number, total, tax, and date, then use known vendor mappings to create an initial recommendation. That gives us rough labels even before the accounting team manually reviews everything.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Exactly. Those labels do not need to be perfect on day one. They just need to be good enough to create a useful first training set, especially for deterministic fields like totals, dates, and invoice numbers.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

And we can also create synthetic samples. We can generate fake OCR-like invoice text for vendors such as Staples, Uber, Adobe, and Comcast, then automatically generate the correct JSON output for training.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Yes, that is one of the most practical ways to bootstrap a LoRA. We can begin with maybe 30 carefully written seed samples, expand with synthetic documents, and then keep improving with real correction logs from production use.

Senior Accountant (Hana):

The correction log will be extremely valuable. If accounting keeps changing “AMZN Mktp US” to “Amazon,” or changes a suggested account from Meals to Office Supplies, that becomes our best real-world learning signal.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Exactly. That is why production feedback matters so much. Over time, the most useful dataset is not synthetic data alone, but the combination of synthetic samples, pseudo-labels, and accountant-approved corrections.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

From an implementation standpoint, we can keep the training project clean. We will have raw OCR exports, processed train-valid-test JSONL files, a synthetic generator, and separate scripts for training, evaluation, inference, and schema validation.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Yes, and the training stack can be straightforward. We use a base instruct model, apply LoRA with PEFT, train with chat-formatted JSONL, and evaluate not just loss but exact-match accuracy on vendor, date, total, and document type.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

That evaluation approach matters. For accounting operations, the meaningful question is not whether the model sounds fluent, but whether it extracted the right vendor, the right amount, and the right document class.

PM (Mina):

Then the three-month roadmap also becomes clearer. Month 1 is reading documents, Month 2 is understanding documents, and Month 3 is turning reviewed results into posting-ready accounting actions.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

In Month 1, we should focus on upload, OCR, document storage, and a document detail screen. Users need to see the original file, raw OCR text, and extracted fields in one place.

AI Engineer (Alex):

In Month 2, we connect Llama and the first extraction LoRA. That is where we add vendor normalization, document classification, suggested account, recommended QuickBooks entity, and confidence-based review routing.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

In Month 3, we need controlled accounting workflow. Draft, Review, Approved, and Posted states should exist, with duplicate detection, audit logs, and QuickBooks-compatible posting only after approval.

PM (Mina):

We should also keep the MVP narrow. Vendor bills, receipts, utility bills, USD only, and core objects like Bill, Expense, and Vendor are enough for the first version.

AI Engineer (Alex):

I agree. We should not attempt payroll, multi-currency, or full ERP logic in the first release. A narrow workflow with strong extraction, review, and posting is much more realistic.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

And technically, that narrower scope makes the platform safer. We can validate JSON outputs, detect duplicates, track audit history, and prevent accidental double posting without overcomplicating the first release.

Senior Accountant (Hana):

From the accounting side, that is the right balance. AI should reduce manual entry and improve document handling speed, but the books should still be controlled by policy, review, and traceable approval.

PM (Mina):

Then our strategy is clear. We are not chasing a famous prebuilt U.S. accounting LoRA. We are building our own practical system: OCR plus a narrow LoRA plus accounting rules plus review plus QuickBooks-style posting.

AI Engineer (Alex):

Exactly. India-specific LoRA models stand out because they package a very specific regional problem into one visible model. In the U.S., the stronger path for us is to build a product-oriented pipeline and train task-specific LoRAs that fit our workflow.

Accounting Lead (Jisoo):

And that is easier to trust from a finance perspective. AI helps us understand documents, but accounting decisions still stay grounded in rules, mappings, and approvals.

Backend Engineer (Daniel):

It is also easier to maintain. Instead of one black-box model trying to do everything, each layer handles a clear responsibility and can be improved independently.

PM (Mina):

Great. Then we will lock the plan this way: start with a 30-sample JSONL seed set, expand with synthetic training data, collect correction logs from real reviews, and grow the LoRA over time while the product ships.

AI Engineer (Alex):

That is the right order. First, build a usable OCR-accounting workflow. Then use that workflow to generate better data. Then train better LoRAs from the data the product creates.

Senior Accountant (Hana):

From my side, that sounds realistic. If the first version can save us from retyping vendor names, totals, taxes, and document categories, the accounting team will already see real value.
