Transfer Pricing & Cost Allocation

Finance Team Meeting

CFO: I was reviewing our global tax report this morning. Something about the profit distribution caught my attention.

Finance Analyst: I noticed that too. A large portion of the profit is recorded in Country A.

Tax Manager: That's because the sales entity is located there. Our current transfer price gives most of the margin to that company.

CFO: But our manufacturing subsidiary is in Country B, right?

Finance Analyst: Yes, and Country B actually has a much lower corporate tax rate.

Tax Manager: Exactly. Country A's tax rate is around 30%, while Country B's is closer to 10%.

CFO: That explains why our tax bill is so high in Country A.

Finance Analyst: If we adjusted the transfer price, the profit distribution would change.

Tax Manager: The manufacturing subsidiary could report higher profit, while the sales company reports less.

CFO: But the total group profit would stay the same.

Finance Analyst: Correct. We would only be changing where the profit appears.

Tax Manager: The key concern is compliance with the arm’s length principle.

CFO: Meaning the price must be similar to what independent companies would charge?

Tax Manager: Exactly. Tax authorities expect internal transactions to resemble market transactions.

Finance Analyst: We could support the pricing with benchmarking studies.

CFO: That would reduce the risk of a tax adjustment.

Tax Manager: Yes, and it would also help if we document the pricing methodology clearly.

CFO: Alright. Let's run some scenarios and see how the numbers change.

Cost Allocation Discussion

Finance Analyst: There's another issue related to our internal reporting.

CFO: What kind of issue?

Finance Analyst: Most headquarters costs are currently recorded in the parent company.

Tax Manager: That includes management salaries, IT systems, and global marketing expenses.

CFO: But those services support several subsidiaries.

Finance Analyst: Exactly. That's why some companies allocate those costs across business units.

Tax Manager: It's called cost allocation.

CFO: What allocation methods are typically used?

Tax Manager: Revenue share, employee headcount, or operational usage are common methods.

Finance Analyst: For example, if a subsidiary generates 30% of the group revenue, it might absorb 30% of certain overhead costs.

CFO: That would reduce the reported profit of that subsidiary.

Finance Analyst: Yes, but the total group cost remains unchanged.

Tax Manager: The key is to choose a reasonable and consistent allocation method.

CFO: And I assume we need to document the rationale carefully.

Tax Manager: Absolutely. That documentation is important during audits.

CFO: Alright. Let's review our current allocation policy next week.
