ITAC Interface Testing Report (Non-Reliance)

This report documents an end-to-end audit workpaper for an automated interface: Shopify / Stripe → Synder → QuickBooks. The audit approach is Non-Reliance (i.e., no reliance on Synder controls / no SOC reliance), and focuses on direct source-to-GL validation.

ITAC Non-Reliance Interface Completeness Interface Accuracy Duplicate & Cutoff Material Weakness Scenario
Scope Summary:
We tested whether transactions originating from Shopify and Stripe are complete, accurate, non-duplicative, and appropriately classified when recorded in QuickBooks via Synder. This report includes sampling methodology, 10 sample tie-outs, exception analysis, expanded testing approach, and severity assessment leading to a Material Weakness conclusion (scenario-based).

1. Background & System Description

Management uses Synder as an integration platform to transmit sales, refunds, fees, and payouts from Shopify and Stripe to QuickBooks Online. The interface is system-driven (API/webhook-based), and transactions are posted to the general ledger through system-generated entries (e.g., sales receipts, deposits, expenses, refund receipts).

  • Upstream: Shopify (orders/refunds/taxes/shipping) and Stripe (charges/fees/payouts)
  • Middleware: Synder (mapping, transformation, sync scheduling, error queues)
  • Downstream: QuickBooks (GL posting, bank feeds, reconciliation)

2. Audit Strategy (Non-Reliance)

We did not place reliance on Synder’s controls. Accordingly, we performed direct testing of completeness and accuracy by reconciling source transactions to amounts recorded in QuickBooks.

Non-Reliance Implication:
Without reliance on Synder controls, assurance is obtained primarily through source-to-QuickBooks tie-outs, population-level reconciliations, expanded testing when deviations occur, and evaluation of compensating controls.

3. Control Objective

To determine whether transactions originating in Shopify and Stripe are:

  • Complete (all relevant transactions recorded in QBO)
  • Accurate (gross, fee, net, and attributes match source)
  • Non-duplicative (unique IDs not posted twice)
  • Properly classified (appropriate GL accounts)
  • Properly cut off (recorded in the appropriate reporting period)

4. Population

Population obtained from management for the audit period:

  • Period: [Jan 1 – Dec 31, 20XX]
  • Total transactions: [18,742]
  • Population sources: Shopify order/refund exports; Stripe balance/payout exports; QBO transaction detail exports

5. Sampling Methodology

5.1 Non-Statistical (Judgmental) Sampling Rationale

We selected a judgmental sample of 10 items focusing on higher-risk characteristics:

  • High-dollar sales
  • Period-end activity (cutoff risk)
  • Refunds / returns
  • Items occurring near suspected configuration changes
  • Unusual amounts (negative, multiple line components)

If desired, a mixed approach may be used (e.g., 15 random + 10 risk-based). In this report, we apply a risk-based sample of 10 to illustrate a material deficiency scenario and expansion logic.

5.2 Selection Documentation

Selection Type Criteria Planned Count
High Value Top amounts by gross sales 4
Period End Within ±5 days of month/quarter-end 3
Refunds Refund transactions / negative gross 2
Unusual / Exceptions Unusual fee, net, or sync log flag 1

6. Test Procedures Performed

For each selected sample, we performed the following:

  • Agreed unique identifier (Shopify Order ID / Stripe Charge ID / Synder Sync ID) to QuickBooks reference
  • Traced and agreed Gross, Fee, and Net amounts from source → QBO
  • Inspected posting date for cutoff
  • Verified GL account classification (Sales, Merchant Fees, Stripe Clearing, Refunds, Sales Tax)
  • Inspected QBO transaction list to identify duplicates for the same unique identifier
Key tie-out rule (eCommerce pattern):
Gross = Sales components (product + shipping + tax if applicable)
Net = Gross − processing fees (Stripe) − other adjustments (e.g., refunds)
Payouts should reconcile to bank deposits, typically via a clearing account.

8. Exception Summary & Interpretation

  • Completeness: Missing sales transaction(s) in QuickBooks
  • Accuracy: Fee/net discrepancies suggesting calculation or mapping issues
  • Cutoff: Period-end sales posted in subsequent period without documented policy/controls
  • Classification: Revenue misposted to clearing account (financial statement presentation risk)
  • Duplicates: Same ID recorded twice (overstatement risk)
Why these matter:
These exception types affect Revenue, Expenses, Cash/Clearing, and Liabilities and, if systematic, can create material misstatements at scale.

9. Root Cause Discussion (Inquiry with Management)

Management indicated that:

  • Synder configuration updates were performed around [date].
  • Monitoring of sync failures is informal (no daily documented review of error queue).
  • No documented review/approval workflow exists for mapping changes.

No automated detective control (e.g., daily source total to GL total reconciliation) was identified that would timely prevent or detect missing/duplicate/misclassified transactions.

10. Expanded Testing Method (Fail Response)

Because deviations suggested potential systematic issues, we performed expanded testing to determine whether the exceptions were isolated or pervasive.

10.1 Expansion Approach

  • Attribute-based expansion (preferred): test 100% of transactions sharing the same attribute (date range, product type, refund type, or config-change window).
  • High-value expansion: test additional high-dollar items where risk of material impact is higher.
  • Additional random selections: increase sample size if no dominant attribute emerges.
Expansion principle:
If the deviation appears systematic (config/mapping logic), attribute-based 100% testing provides the strongest audit evidence and is often more efficient than simply adding random samples.

10.2 Expanded Testing Performed (Scenario)

  • Additional items tested: 15 (focused on the suspected configuration change window and high-value sales)
  • Additional deviations identified: 6
  • Deviation pattern: recurring fee mismatch + intermittent missing IDs + occasional duplicates

11. Compensating Controls Considered

We assessed whether management performs controls that would detect these issues timely, including:

  • Daily/weekly reconciliation between Stripe payouts and QBO bank deposits
  • Daily sales summary review (Shopify vs QBO)
  • Stripe clearing reconciliation (clearing balance should return to zero)
  • Exception/error queue monitoring in Synder

Based on inquiry and evidence inspected, management’s reviews were high-level and would not reliably detect:

  • Missing individual orders
  • Duplicate postings by unique ID
  • Misclassification of revenue vs clearing
Conclusion on compensating controls:
Compensating controls were assessed as not operating effectively to prevent or detect the identified deviations.

12. Likelihood & Impact Assessment (Severity Framework)

12.1 Likelihood

  • Assessment: High
  • Rationale: High deviation rate in initial sample (5/10) and continued deviations in expanded testing (6/15). The deviation types suggest recurring systemic conditions rather than one-off errors.

12.2 Impact

  • Assessment: High
  • Rationale: Exceptions directly impact revenue and cash-related balances. Missing, duplicate, and misclassified postings can produce material misstatements across multiple accounts and periods.

13. Severity Determination (Deficiency vs Significant vs Material)

Severity is determined based on Likelihood × Impact and the presence/absence of effective compensating controls.

Severity Level General Meaning Typical Indicators in an Interface
Control Deficiency Improvement needed; low risk of material misstatement Isolated error, small amounts, quickly corrected, detective controls catch it
Significant Deficiency Warrants governance attention Recurring errors within a subset (product/period), moderate impact, compensating controls partially effective
Material Weakness Reasonable possibility of material misstatement not prevented/detected Missing/duplicate postings, pervasive mapping issues, multi-period exposure, lack of effective monitoring controls
Severity Conclusion (Scenario):
Given the high likelihood (frequent deviations across multiple types), high impact (revenue/cash exposure), and absence of effective compensating controls, the deficiency is evaluated as a Material Weakness.

14. Overall Conclusion

Based on the procedures performed, the automated interface facilitated through Synder is assessed as not operating effectively to ensure completeness and accuracy of transaction recording in QuickBooks.

  • Initial sample testing identified 5/10 deviations (completeness, accuracy, cutoff, classification, duplication).
  • Expanded testing identified additional deviations, supporting a systemic issue assessment.
  • Compensating controls were not sufficient to prevent or detect the issues in a timely manner.

Accordingly, we conclude this represents a Material Weakness in internal control over financial reporting for the interface process (scenario-based).

15. Audit Implications & Recommended Actions

15.1 Audit Implications

  • Increase substantive procedures over revenue, merchant fees, and cash/clearing balances.
  • Perform additional period-end procedures to address cutoff risk.
  • Consider full-period roll-forward/roll-back tests around configuration change windows.

15.2 Management Remediation Recommendations

  • Implement formal change management and approvals for Synder mappings/configuration.
  • Enable and document daily monitoring of Synder error queues and reprocessing logs.
  • Establish daily/weekly reconciliations: Shopify gross sales ↔ QBO sales; Stripe fees/payouts ↔ QBO deposits and clearing.
  • Restrict access to integration settings; perform periodic access reviews.
  • Define and document cutoff policies (order date vs payout date vs posting date) and enforce in configuration.

Auditor Non-Reliance Approach
Since we are not relying on Synder controls, our assurance must come from direct evidence that source transactions are fully and accurately recorded in QuickBooks. The deviation pattern suggests systemic risk, so we expanded testing and evaluated compensating controls.

Management Response
We recognize the monitoring gap. We will implement daily error-queue review, restrict access to mapping changes, and perform recurring reconciliations between source totals and QuickBooks postings.

Auditor Severity
Without effective monitoring and change governance, missing, duplicate, and misclassified postings can occur at scale. Based on likelihood and impact, the control issue is evaluated as a material weakness in this scenario.
