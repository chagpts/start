International Tax Case Study
— Understanding Subpart F and GILTI

This case study explains two important U.S. international tax concepts: Subpart F and GILTI. Both rules are designed to prevent U.S. multinational groups from shifting income to low-tax foreign subsidiaries and deferring U.S. tax indefinitely.

Scene 1 — Why These Rules Exist

Finance Team Member: I have a basic understanding that Subpart F and GILTI are U.S. international tax concepts, but I still do not understand how they work in practice. Could you explain them in a way that would help someone in finance understand the business impact?

Accountant: Certainly. The best way to understand them is to start with the policy objective. Both Subpart F and GILTI are anti-deferral rules. In simple terms, they are designed to stop U.S. taxpayers from earning income through foreign subsidiaries and indefinitely postponing U.S. tax on that income.

Finance Team Member: When you say "deferral," do you mean that the U.S. parent historically did not pay U.S. tax until the foreign subsidiary actually distributed a dividend?

Accountant: Yes, that is the basic idea. Historically, active business earnings of a foreign subsidiary could often remain outside the U.S. tax net until repatriated. Congress became concerned that U.S. groups could move income into low-tax jurisdictions and keep it there. Subpart F was the earlier response to that concern, and GILTI was later introduced to broaden the reach of the anti-deferral regime.

회계사: 맞습니다. 기본 개념은 그렇습니다. 과거에는 해외 자회사의 적극적 사업소득(active business earnings)이 미국으로 송금되기 전까지는 미국 과세 대상 밖에 머무를 수 있는 경우가 많았습니다. 미국 의회는 미국 기업집단이 소득을 저세율 국가로 이전한 뒤 그곳에 계속 쌓아두는 구조를 우려하게 되었고, 그 우려에 대한 초기 대응이 Subpart F였으며, 이후 그 범위를 더 넓히기 위해 GILTI가 도입되었습니다.

Scene 2 — What Is a CFC?

Finance Team Member: Before we go into the differences, I think I need to understand the basic framework. What type of foreign subsidiary are we talking about?

Accountant: We are generally talking about a Controlled Foreign Corporation, or CFC. Broadly speaking, a CFC is a foreign corporation that is more than 50% owned, by vote or value, by U.S. shareholders. For this purpose, a U.S. shareholder generally means a U.S. person that owns at least 10% of the corporation.

Finance Team Member: So if a U.S. parent company owns 100% of a foreign subsidiary, that subsidiary is very likely a CFC?

Accountant: Correct. In many multinational groups, wholly owned foreign subsidiaries are CFCs, which means the U.S. parent must consider Subpart F and GILTI each year, even if no dividend is paid.

High-Level Comparison

At a high level, the distinction is this: Subpart F primarily targets certain categories of income that are considered mobile or passive, while GILTI is intended to pick up a broader residual amount of foreign earnings, especially earnings that exceed a routine return on tangible assets.

Item Subpart F GILTI
Main purpose Immediate taxation of certain foreign income Current taxation of broader excess foreign earnings
Main focus Passive / mobile income Excess return above routine return on tangible assets
Typical examples Interest, dividends, royalties, certain related-party income Residual business profit of CFCs
Timing Included currently by U.S. shareholder Included currently by U.S. shareholder

Scene 3 — Detailed Explanation of Subpart F

Finance Team Member: Let us start with Subpart F. Why did the law focus specifically on passive or mobile income?

Accountant: Because that type of income is easier to shift artificially. For example, a multinational group can often move intellectual property, financing arrangements, or contract structures into a low-tax jurisdiction more easily than it can move a real factory with employees and operations. As a result, passive and mobile income was seen as especially vulnerable to tax planning.

Finance Team Member: What kinds of income usually fall into Subpart F?

Accountant: At a simplified level, the most common items are interest, dividends, rents, royalties, and certain gains. There are also more technical categories, such as certain related-party sales income and related-party services income. In practice, when a finance team reviews a foreign subsidiary, it often pays close attention to treasury activity, licensing arrangements, and intercompany transactions because those areas can trigger Subpart F.

Typical category Simple explanation
Interest income Return on lending or deposits
Dividend income Income from shareholdings
Royalty income Income from licensing IP
Certain related-party sales income Margin from buying/selling through foreign entities in a tax-planned structure
Certain related-party services income Service income earned through foreign structures for related parties

Scene 4 — Numerical Example of Subpart F

Finance Team Member: Could you give me a practical example that a finance team might actually recognize?

Accountant: Certainly. Suppose a U.S. parent transfers intellectual property rights to a subsidiary in a low-tax jurisdiction. That foreign subsidiary then licenses the IP to operating affiliates around the world and receives royalty income. Even if the cash remains offshore, those royalties may be picked up currently by the U.S. parent under Subpart F, depending on the facts.

Item Amount
Royalty income earned by foreign subsidiary $5,000,000
Interest income from deposits/investments $2,000,000
Local operating income from real business activities $3,000,000
Total pre-tax income $10,000,000

If the $5 million royalty income and $2 million interest income fall within Subpart F, then $7 million may be included currently by the U.S. shareholder, even if no cash distribution is made.

Scene 5 — Why GILTI Was Added

Finance Team Member: If Subpart F already existed, why did the U.S. need GILTI?

Accountant: Because policymakers believed that Subpart F was too narrow. Many multinational groups could still earn large amounts of foreign income in low-tax jurisdictions without triggering Subpart F, especially where the income was structured as operating profit rather than classic passive income. Congress concluded that a broader rule was needed.

Finance Team Member: Can you explain that with a business example?

Accountant: Of course. Imagine a foreign subsidiary in a low-tax jurisdiction that legally owns valuable intangible assets such as software, patents, algorithms, or trademarks. That subsidiary may earn substantial business profits from exploiting those assets. Even if that income is not technically interest or dividends, and therefore may not be classic Subpart F income, the economic reality is that the group may still be concentrating profit in a low-tax location. GILTI was designed to capture part of that outcome.

Korean Translation

재무팀 직원: 이미 Subpart F가 있었는데, 미국은 왜 GILTI를 추가로 도입했나요?

회계사: 정책 입안자들은 Subpart F만으로는 범위가 너무 좁다고 보았기 때문입니다. 많은 다국적 기업집단이 여전히 저세율 국가에서 거액의 해외 소득을 올리면서도 Subpart F를 피할 수 있었고, 특히 그 소득이 전형적인 수동적 소득이 아니라 영업이익의 형태로 구조화된 경우가 그랬습니다. 그래서 의회는 보다 포괄적인 규정이 필요하다고 판단했습니다.

재무팀 직원: 그 부분을 사업 사례로 설명해 주실 수 있을까요?

회계사: 물론입니다. 저세율 국가의 해외 자회사가 소프트웨어, 특허, 알고리즘, 상표와 같은 가치 있는 무형자산을 법적으로 보유하고 있다고 가정해 보겠습니다. 그 자회사는 그러한 자산을 활용해 상당한 사업이익을 올릴 수 있습니다. 그 소득이 기술적으로 이자나 배당은 아니어서 전형적인 Subpart F 소득이 아닐 수는 있지만, 경제적 실질을 보면 기업집단이 이익을 저세율 국가에 집중시키고 있는 것일 수 있습니다. GILTI는 바로 그런 결과의 일부를 포착하기 위해 만들어졌습니다.

Scene 6 — Core Idea of GILTI

Finance Team Member: Then what is the basic logic of GILTI?

Accountant: At a simplified level, GILTI assumes that a CFC should be allowed a routine return on its tangible business assets, such as machinery, equipment, and buildings. The law uses a 10% return on certain tangible assets as a rough benchmark. If the foreign earnings exceed that routine amount, the excess may be treated as GILTI.

Finance Team Member: Why does the rule use tangible assets as the benchmark?

Accountant: Because policymakers viewed very high profits relative to tangible assets as a possible sign that the income was being driven by intangible assets placed in low-tax jurisdictions. In other words, if a foreign company has only a modest amount of plant and equipment but reports very large profits, the law assumes that some of those profits may reflect mobile intangible value rather than routine local operations.

재무팀 직원: 그렇다면 GILTI의 기본 논리는 무엇인가요?

Scene 7 — Numerical Example of GILTI

Finance Team Member: I think a numerical example would help here as well.

Accountant: Certainly. Assume a foreign subsidiary has $100 million of qualifying tangible assets and earns $40 million of tested income for the year. Under the simplified approach, a 10% routine return on those tangible assets would be $10 million. The excess, or $30 million, is the amount that would conceptually fall into the GILTI basket before applying the more detailed tax rules.

Item Amount
Tangible business assets $100,000,000
Tested income $40,000,000
10% routine return on tangible assets $10,000,000
Excess amount $30,000,000

Scene 8 — Relationship Between Subpart F and GILTI

Finance Team Member: How do the two rules interact? Could the same income be taxed twice under both Subpart F and GILTI?

Accountant: As a conceptual matter, Subpart F is generally considered first. Income that is already taken into account under Subpart F is typically excluded from the GILTI calculation. So the system is not intended to impose both regimes on the exact same item of income in the same way.

Finance Team Member: So one way to think about it is that Subpart F picks up certain specifically identified items first, and GILTI then captures part of the broader remaining foreign earnings?

Accountant: Yes, that is a very useful way to think about it at a high level. It is not a complete technical description, but for finance and management purposes, that framework is generally helpful.

회계사: 개념적으로는 일반적으로 Subpart F를 먼저 봅니다. 이미 Subpart F로 반영된 소득은 통상 GILTI 계산에서 제외됩니다. 따라서 제도 자체는 동일한 소득 항목에 대해 두 규정을 완전히 중복 적용하려는 구조는 아닙니다.

회계사: 네, 큰 틀에서는 그렇게 이해하는 것이 매우 유용합니다. 완전한 기술적 설명은 아니지만, 재무나 경영진 관점에서는 그 프레임이 대체로 도움이 됩니다.

Scene 9 — Why Finance Teams Should Care

Finance Team Member: From a finance reporting perspective, why are these concepts so important?

Accountant: They matter because they can create current U.S. tax expense even when cash has not been repatriated. That affects the effective tax rate, tax provision calculations, deferred tax analysis in some cases, earnings forecasting, cash tax planning, and internal discussions about where profits are being generated and how intercompany arrangements are structured.

Finance Team Member: So even if treasury says the cash is still offshore, tax may say we already have a current U.S. inclusion?

Accountant: That is exactly right. From a finance perspective, one of the most important mindset shifts is that legal cash distribution and tax inclusion are not always the same thing. Subpart F and GILTI are both examples of that principle.

One-Minute Closing Explanation

Finance Team Member: If I had to explain this to another person in the finance department in one minute, how should I say it?

Accountant: You could say it this way: Subpart F and GILTI are U.S. tax rules that cause a U.S. parent to recognize certain foreign subsidiary income currently, even without a dividend. Subpart F mainly targets passive or easily shifted income, such as interest and royalties. GILTI is broader and generally targets foreign profits that are considered too high relative to the subsidiary's tangible assets, which may indicate profit concentration in low-tax jurisdictions.

Finance Team Member: That explanation is very helpful. I now see that Subpart F is more category-driven, while GILTI is more of a broader residual test on foreign earnings.

Accountant: Exactly. That is a strong summary. If you keep that distinction in mind, most of the detailed rules will make more sense as you continue reviewing the tax materials.

Practical Takeaways for Finance Teams

  • Do not assume cash repatriation and tax inclusion happen at the same time.
  • For Subpart F, review the character of income. Interest, royalties, dividends, and certain intercompany transactions are key risk areas.
  • For GILTI, review profitability relative to tangible assets. High earnings with a small fixed-asset base may create exposure.
  • Tax and finance should coordinate early. These rules can affect current tax expense, ETR, forecasting, and tax provision workpapers.

Additional Technical Note (Hidden)

Possible Advanced Topics to Expand Later (Click to Expand)

The explanation above is intentionally simplified for finance training purposes. In real corporate tax work, the analysis can become much more technical.

  • E&P (Earnings and Profits): U.S. tax inclusions often depend on tax-based E&P concepts rather than book income alone.
  • Tested Income / Tested Loss: GILTI uses tested income mechanics rather than a simple accounting profit test.
  • QBAI: The routine return concept is tied to qualified business asset investment, which is more technical than a plain fixed-asset balance.
  • FTC (Foreign Tax Credits): Foreign taxes can materially affect the overall tax cost of GILTI and Subpart F inclusions.
  • Entity-by-entity facts matter: Intercompany pricing, local tax rates, tangible asset profile, and legal ownership of IP can all change the outcome.

These are often the next topics covered when a tax team explains Subpart F and GILTI in more depth to finance leadership.
