3. Entity Form Comparison: LLC vs C-Corp

A Delaware business can be operated primarily as:

  • C-Corp (U.S. domestic corporation)
  • LLC (potentially foreign-owned pass-through / disregarded entity)
Study note: These two forms can lead to entirely different U.S. tax results. A C-Corp is typically taxed on worldwide income. A foreign-owned LLC may have zero U.S. tax if there is no U.S. Trade or Business (USTB), no Effectively Connected Income (ECI), and no relevant PE under the treaty.

4. C-Corporation Structure Review

4.1 Core Tax Principle

A Delaware C-Corp is a U.S. domestic corporation. In general:

  • Customer nationality (foreign vs U.S.) does not remove U.S. corporate tax.
  • Even if the service is delivered globally via the internet, the corporation is usually taxed on worldwide income.

U.S. federal corporate income tax rate commonly referenced: 21% (IRC §11).

4.2 Example Tax Computation (Profit = USD 500,000)

Step 1 — U.S. Corporate Tax

Taxable profit: 500,000
Federal corporate tax rate: 21%
→ Corporate tax = 500,000 × 21% = 105,000

Step 2 — Dividend Withholding Tax (U.S. → Korea)

After-tax profit = 500,000 − 105,000 = 395,000

  • Default U.S. dividend withholding: 30%
  • Assuming U.S.–Korea tax treaty applies: reduced to 15%

→ Withholding tax = 395,000 × 15% = 59,250

Category Amount (USD)
Corporate tax 105,000
Dividend withholding 59,250
Total U.S. tax 164,250
Net cash received by Korean individual 335,750

4.3 Key Characteristics (Pros / Cons)

Advantages

  • Clear, conventional U.S. tax structure (21% + dividend withholding)
  • Common and investor-friendly structure (familiar for U.S. funding)

Disadvantages

  • “Zero U.S. tax” is not feasible (U.S. domestic corporation pays U.S. corporate tax)
  • Two layers of tax: corporate-level tax + shareholder-level withholding on dividends

5. LLC Pass-Through Structure Review

5.1 Core Tax Principle

If a Delaware LLC is:

  • Single-member
  • Owned by a foreign person (e.g., Korean individual)
  • Treated as a disregarded entity for U.S. tax

Then the LLC itself is typically not taxed at the entity level. Instead, the foreign owner may be taxed only if there is:

  • U.S. Trade or Business (USTB), and/or
  • Effectively Connected Income (ECI), and/or
  • Permanent Establishment (PE) considerations under the treaty

Single-member vs Multi-member LLC (Why it matters)

  • Single-member LLC: By default, it is treated as a disregarded entity (attributed to the owner). In other words, the key issue is whether there is USTB/ECI/PE at the owner level, not at the “LLC entity itself” level.
  • Multi-member LLC: By default, it is classified as a partnership (separate reporting structure / partner allocation concept). U.S. taxation, withholding, and filing issues can become more complex for each member/partner.
  • For both, in principle, the “21% corporate tax at the company level like a C-Corp” does not automatically apply (however, it can differ depending on elections and specific facts/circumstances).

Study reminder: An LLC’s “tax classification” can vary depending on member count, elections, and facts/circumstances, so actual application requires professional confirmation, including the appropriate filing form.

Key study takeaway: Under certain conditions (no USTB/ECI/PE), a foreign-owned LLC may achieve 0 U.S. income tax on business profits. This is highly facts-and-implementation dependent.

5.3 Service / Cloud Income Sourcing (Study Note)

U.S. tax sourcing for “services” is commonly summarized as:

Service income is sourced to where the services are performed.

For cloud transactions (e.g., certain cloud computing / digital service arrangements), sourcing characterization can become more nuanced than a simple “where performed” summary. If you want a study reference for how Treasury/IRS discusses sourcing rules in the cloud context, see: Federal Register — Source of Income From Cloud Transactions (2025-01-14) .

Study framing: In this document, we use a simplified assumption of “automated SaaS + operated outside the U.S.”, but in practice, depending on the facts—such as transaction type (cloud transaction characterization), place of performance, and the location of servers/people/decision-making—income sourcing and U.S. tax issues can vary.

If operations are conducted in Korea and the company has:

  • No U.S. employees
  • No U.S. office
  • No U.S. contracting or sales activities

then the income may be treated as non-U.S.-source and potentially not taxable in the U.S. (subject to classification and PE analysis).

5.4 AWS (U.S. Region) and PE Discussion

What is PE?

A Permanent Establishment (PE) is generally a “fixed place of business” under tax treaties. If a PE exists in the U.S., the U.S. may tax business profits attributable to that PE.

Can a Server Create PE?

  • A website by itself is not a PE.
  • A server may be a PE if the enterprise controls the server and it plays a core role in conducting the business.

This concept is commonly discussed in OECD commentary and treaty practice.

Is AWS Hosting a PE?

  • Typical SaaS users do not own the physical server.
  • AWS is generally a hosted cloud service (rental/usage model).

Therefore, many practical interpretations conclude: cloud hosting alone generally does not create a PE.

⚠ However, if the server infrastructure performs core revenue-generating functions and the facts suggest “control,” PE arguments may still arise. It’s not always a guaranteed conclusion.

Related reading for study (cloud transaction sourcing discussion): Federal Register — Source of Income From Cloud Transactions

8. Final Conclusions

8.1 Foreign customers do NOT automatically mean “zero U.S. tax”

U.S. tax conclusions depend on:

  • Whether there is a U.S. Trade or Business (USTB)
  • Whether income is ECI
  • Whether a PE exists under treaty analysis

Customer nationality is usually not the controlling factor.

8.2 C-Corp cannot be “U.S. tax zero”

A U.S. domestic corporation is generally taxed at the corporate level on worldwide income. Therefore, achieving “0 U.S. corporate tax” is not feasible in a normal C-Corp setup.

8.3 LLC may be “U.S. tax zero” — but conditional

  • No U.S. employees / office ✅
  • Operations and service performance outside U.S. ✅
  • AWS U.S. servers may raise PE discussions ⚠️
  • Single-member vs multi-member classification differences can change compliance complexity ⚠️

The LLC approach can be very tax-efficient, but is not “automatically guaranteed.” It requires deliberate operational design and risk management.

8.4 Practical “Most Defensible” Option (if targeting minimal U.S. tax)

  1. Maintain LLC pass-through / disregarded status (when applicable)
  2. Move AWS infrastructure outside the U.S. (reduce PE arguments)
  3. Strictly avoid U.S.-based staff, office, contract execution, and sales operations
  4. Report and pay taxes properly in Korea under Korean individual taxation rules
